**Taxonomy and Evolution:**

The platypus (Ornithorhynchus anatinus) belongs to the monotreme order, which includes only five extant species and is characterized by laying eggs instead of giving birth to live young. Monotremes are considered evolutionary relics, representing an ancient lineage that diverged from other mammals approximately 166 million years ago.

**Physical Description:**

The platypus is instantly recognizable by its unique appearance, resembling a whimsical amalgamation of different animals. It has a sleek, streamlined body covered in dense, waterproof fur, which varies in color from dark brown to lighter shades. Its most striking features include a broad, flat tail resembling that of a beaver, webbed feet perfect for aquatic propulsion, and a distinctive duck-like bill.

**Bill and Sensory Abilities:**

The bill of the platypus is perhaps its most remarkable feature. It is soft, rubbery, and packed with sensory receptors known as electroreceptors, enabling the platypus to detect the faint electrical signals emitted by its prey underwater. This electrolocation ability allows it to hunt effectively even in murky waters where visibility is limited.

**Venomous Spurs:**

Male platypuses possess venomous spurs on their hind legs, a feature that sets them apart from females. These spurs are connected to venom glands and are primarily used during mating season and territorial disputes. While the venom is not lethal to humans, it can cause excruciating pain and swelling.
